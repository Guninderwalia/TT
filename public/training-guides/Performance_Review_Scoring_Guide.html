Performance Review Scoring Guide

How TaskTango calculates each employee's Overall Score, in plain English. Updated for Production v3.5.

🆕 What's New in v3.5

  • Last Increment column removed. Salary-increment history is admin-only context and now lives only inside the Edit Employee modal. The table is cleaner without it.
  • Employee filter sits after Department, so you can pull up one person's KPIs at a time instead of scanning the whole list.
  • Department filter fixed. The previous version silently excluded everyone when a department was selected (an ID-type mismatch). It now returns just that department, as expected.
  • The scoring math itself hasn't changed — same six components, same weights — only the table UI did.

The Overall Score at a Glance

Every employee's Overall Score is a single number out of 100, made up of six parts. Each part is worth a different share of the total — the bigger the share, the more it pushes the Overall Score up or down.

# What it measures Share of the
Overall Score		 How an employee improves it
1 ⭐Skills 40% Get high ratings on individual skills (communication, teamwork, technical expertise, etc.) during skill assessments.
2 👤Manager Rating 20% Receive a high star rating (out of 5) from their direct manager during the review period.
3 📅Attendance 10% Show up for work. Approved leave days don't hurt this score — only unexcused absences do.
4 ⏰Punctuality 10% Sign in on or before their scheduled start time on the days they're present.
5 📊Consistency 10% Log a full 8-hour day in Time Logging, regularly.
6 🚦Lateness 10% Keep late minutes low across the period (the fewer minutes late, the higher the score).
TOTAL 100% —
Quick takeaway: 60% of an employee's score comes from quality measures (Skills + Manager Rating). The other 40% comes from attendance and time-keeping measures, split evenly into four 10% slices.

How Each Metric is Calculated

⭐Skills — 40%

What it showsAverage of all the employee's individual skill ratings (each 1–5 stars), scaled to a score out of 100.
Top score (100)Every skill rated 5 out of 5.
Half score (50)Skills averaging 2.5 out of 5.
Zero (0)No skill assessments on record at all.
Key pointThis is the biggest single factor — an employee with strong skill assessments has a 40-point head start before anything else is counted.

👤Manager Rating — 20%

What it showsThe most recent star rating (1–5) that the employee's manager has given them.
Scale ★★★★★ = 100
★★★★ ☆ = 80
★★★ ☆☆ = 60
★★ ☆☆☆ = 40
★ ☆☆☆☆ = 20
No review yet = 0
Key pointAn un-reviewed employee can't score above 60% overall even with perfect attendance — managers should make sure reviews are kept current.

📅Attendance — 10%

What it showsThe percentage of working days the employee was either at work OR on an approved leave (anything except "Absent").
Top score (100)Marked as Present or Leave on every working day in the period.
What hurts itDays marked Absent, or working days with no attendance record at all.
What does NOT hurt itApproved leave days — these now count as attended (taking holiday won't drop your score).
SundaysNot counted as working days, so they don't affect the score either way.

⏰Punctuality — 10%

What it showsOf the days the employee was actually present (not on leave), the percentage where they signed in on or before their scheduled start time.
Top score (100)On time every single day they were at work.
How "on time" is judgedSign-in time compared against the employee's own scheduled start time (e.g. 09:00). Each employee can have a different start time.
MaximumCapped at 100% — no score can exceed it.

📊Consistency — 10%

What it showsHow close the employee's average logged hours are to a standard 8-hour day.
Top score (100)Averaging 8+ hours of logged work per day.
Half score (50)Averaging 4 hours per day.
What's countedOnly days where the employee actually logged time. Days with no log don't drag the score down — they just aren't included in the average.

🚦Lateness — 10%

What it showsA penalty-style score: the more minutes late across the period, the lower the score.
Top score (100)Never late, or always logging on or before scheduled start time.
What hurts itEvery minute past the scheduled start time accumulates, then is divided across total working minutes — the larger that ratio, the lower the score.
No time logs?Score is 0 (we can't grade lateness without a baseline of logged hours).

Worked Example

Here's what a typical employee with strong but imperfect performance would look like:

Metric Raw result Score (out of 100) Weight Points added
⭐ Skills 4.0 / 5 average skill rating 80 × 40% 32.00
👤 Manager Rating 4 out of 5 stars 80 × 20% 16.00
📅 Attendance 19 of 20 working days attended (incl. 2 days approved leave) 95 × 10% 9.50
⏰ Punctuality 16 of 19 present days on time 84 × 10% 8.42
📊 Consistency Avg 7.8 hrs / logged day 98 × 10% 9.75
🚦 Lateness 3.2% lateness impact 97 × 10% 9.68
OVERALL SCORE
85.35
32.00 + 16.00 + 9.50 + 8.42 + 9.75 + 9.68

Reading the Score

Badge Range Interpretation
Green 80–100 Strong performance. Skills, attendance, and time-keeping are all working well.
Yellow 60–79 Acceptable but with clear room to improve. Look at which individual metrics are dragging it down.
Red 0–59 Needs attention. Often a sign that either skills assessments / manager reviews are missing, or attendance is poor.

Things Worth Knowing

🟢 Approved leave days don't penalise the employee. Annual leave or any non-absent status counts toward the Attendance score the same as a present day.
⚠️ No data means no points. If an employee has no records at all (no attendance, no time logs, no review, no skills), the Overall Score is 0 — not an artificial baseline.
🎯 Quality outweighs quantity. Skills (40%) plus Manager Rating (20%) together drive 60% of the score. Keep skill assessments and reviews current — they have the most influence.
📈 Everything is capped at 100. Each individual metric is bounded between 0 and 100, so the Overall Score can never exceed 100.
🕒 Time logs power two metrics. Both Consistency and Lateness rely on the Time Logging entries. Employees who don't log time will score 0 on both — even if their attendance is perfect.
