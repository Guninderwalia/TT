1. Why we review at all

A performance review is not a punishment, not a salary lever, and not a paperwork exercise. It exists to do three things, in order of importance:

  1. Tell the employee where they actually stand. Most people have a slightly wrong picture of how they're seen by their team — too positive or too negative. A review corrects that.
  2. Give the employee something concrete to improve. If they leave the conversation knowing "I'm strong at X, I need to work on Y" they have a path forward.
  3. Create a paper trail. A year from now, when promotion, raise, or (rarely) termination conversations happen, you'll want documented context.
A score is just a number. The comments are the part the employee remembers in five years. Spend 80 % of your effort on the comments, 20 % on the scoring.

2. The 1–5 scale and what each number means

TaskTango uses a five-point scale. Anchor each score to a behaviour, not a feeling.

ScoreLabelMeaning in plain English
1 Below expectations Consistently misses what the role requires. Needs immediate, structured support or this isn't the right role.
2 Developing Hits the basics but gaps are visible. New joiners often score here for the first 3–6 months.
3 Meets expectations Solid. Does the job they were hired to do. This is the normal, healthy score.
4 Exceeds expectations Regularly delivers above the bar. Mentors others. The kind of person you'd want more of.
5 Outstanding Sets a new bar for the team. Maybe 1 in 10 reviews. Reserve this so it means something.
3/5 is not a polite way of saying "I have a problem with you". It means doing the job well. Calibrate your team around 3 as the normal and adjust up or down based on real evidence.

3. The eight default categories explained

① Punctuality & attendance

Did they show up on time, sign in promptly, log breaks honestly, take leave with notice?

1

Frequent late sign-ins, last-minute leave, no-shows

3

On time most days, gives reasonable notice for leave

5

Never late, plans leave well ahead, covers for others

Data source: Attendance + Time Log totals over the period.

② Quality of work

Does what they deliver hold up? Few defects, few re-do's, the user/customer gets what they expected?

1

Frequent re-work needed, multiple complaints

3

Output is reliable; occasional minor fixes

5

Sets a quality bar others copy; rare to find issues

Data source: your own observation + customer/peer feedback.

③ Productivity

Volume of meaningful output. Not "hours at the desk" — actual completed work.

1

Significantly behind peers in similar role

3

Keeps pace with the team's normal throughput

5

Out-delivers peers without sacrificing quality

④ Communication

Clarity in writing and speaking. Responds to chat in reasonable time. Doesn't go silent on important threads.

1

Misunderstood often; ghosts on chats

3

Clear, replies within a few hours

5

Writes documents others learn from; great in meetings

⑤ Teamwork & collaboration

Plays well with peers. Helps when asked. Doesn't create silos.

1

Friction with colleagues; HR escalations

3

Pleasant to work with; pulls weight in group tasks

5

Improves morale; people request to work with them

⑥ Initiative

Volunteers ideas. Spots problems before being told. Doesn't wait to be assigned everything.

1

Strictly task-list driven; no proactive moves

3

Occasionally proposes improvements

5

Drives substantial improvements without prompting

⑦ Learning & growth

Picks up new skills, asks good questions, improves over time.

1

Same gaps as last review; no growth visible

3

Steady upskilling; takes feedback on board

5

Mastered a new domain this period; mentors others

⑧ Adherence to process & policy

Uses the systems we agreed (TaskTango itself, ticket trackers, change control). Doesn't bypass for convenience.

1

Bypasses process repeatedly; causes incidents

3

Follows process; suggests improvements through channels

5

Helps document and improve the team's processes

4. Worked examples — three reviews end-to-end

Example A — Solid mid-career employee, 18 months in role
Punctuality 4 · Quality 4 · Productivity 3 · Communication 4 · Teamwork 4 · Initiative 3 · Learning 3 · Process 4 → Average 3.6
Strengths: "Has been the team's go-to person for client demos this half. Picked up the new pricing module quickly and now owns it end-to-end. Demos consistently get positive feedback in Slack."
Areas to improve: "Productivity dipped in April when juggling demos with backlog work — we should agree a clearer split on your time."
Overall: "Reliable senior contributor heading into the next half. Let's plan one stretch goal — your choice — for next quarter."
Example B — Struggling new joiner, 4 months in
Punctuality 3 · Quality 2 · Productivity 2 · Communication 3 · Teamwork 4 · Initiative 2 · Learning 3 · Process 3 → Average 2.75
Strengths: "Great cultural fit — pairs well with Asha, gets along with everyone. Asks thoughtful questions in stand-up."
Areas to improve: "Output quality has needed several reviews each sprint — needs a clearer definition-of-done before starting tasks. Productivity will improve as quality improves (less re-work). Suggest pairing with Vikram for the next sprint."
Overall: "Promising start, but quality must come up over the next 2 months to clear probation. Let's check in fortnightly."
Example C — Exceptional senior, 4 years in
Punctuality 5 · Quality 5 · Productivity 4 · Communication 5 · Teamwork 5 · Initiative 5 · Learning 4 · Process 5 → Average 4.75
Strengths: "Quietly rewrote the deployment pipeline this half — cut release time from 45 min to 8 min. Mentored two new joiners through their probation. Document quality is a benchmark for the rest of us."
Areas to improve: "Productivity dipped slightly because so much time went into mentoring — that's the right trade-off but let's plan it explicitly next half rather than absorbing it ad-hoc."
Overall: "Standout half. Promotion discussion to follow this review."

5. Writing comments that land well

Use specific evidence, not generic praise

❌ Vague✅ Specific
"Great team player""Covered Asha's client during her two-week leave without missing a single deadline"
"Needs to improve communication""Three project threads went silent for 3+ days in March — suggest defaulting to a daily status note on active threads"
"Hard worker""Sign-in records show 95 % punctuality across the half; closed 47 tickets vs the team median of 38"

Frame growth, don't assign blame

"You're terrible at estimates" → "Estimates have run over in 4 of the last 5 projects — let's experiment with splitting work into smaller chunks to make estimation more reliable."

Lead with strengths

People absorb the first 30 seconds of feedback well. Lead with what's working before what isn't. By the time you get to areas-to-improve they're listening, not defensive.

6. Common biases to watch for

Recency bias

Whatever happened in the last two weeks looms larger than the previous five months. Cure: take notes monthly so the review draws from the whole period.

Halo / horns effect

One brilliant deliverable boosts every score; one big mistake tanks every score. Cure: score each category against its own evidence, in isolation.

Central tendency

Giving everyone 3s to avoid hard conversations. Cure: force yourself to use the 2 and 4 columns where evidence supports it.

Similar-to-me bias

Rating people who work like you do higher than people who solve problems differently. Cure: scoring criteria, not vibes.

Inflation

Rounding everyone up so nobody feels bad. Cure: if your team averages 4.5, your scale has lost meaning — and people who really earn 5s are no longer recognised.

7. How to enter the review in TaskTango

  1. Sidebar → Performance Reviews.
  2. Find the employee. Click + New Review.
  3. Pick the review period (e.g. 1 Jan – 30 Jun 2026).
  4. For each category, pick a number 1–5. Hover the category label for the anchor description.
  5. Fill in Strengths, Areas to improve, Overall comments.
  6. Click Save Review.
  7. Click Download PDF if you want a copy. The PDF includes the reviewer's name (you, as Team Lead) and a clean signature block.

8. Delivering the review face-to-face

Never publish a review without talking it through first. The published PDF should contain no surprises.

  1. Schedule a 30-min 1:1 (use the TaskTango video call — minimize works so you can both see notes).
  2. Open with what's going well — your top two strengths from the review.
  3. Walk through the scores in order. Let them respond.
  4. Cover the areas-to-improve as a conversation, not a verdict.
  5. Agree the one or two things they'll focus on next half.
  6. Save the review in TaskTango. Tell them to expect the PDF in their My Performance view.
If feedback is going to be difficult, write the review first, sit on it overnight, re-read it next morning before delivering. Anything that sounds harsh in the cold light of morning gets reworded.

9. FAQ

Q: How often should reviews happen?

Half-yearly is standard. Quarterly if your company moves fast or has lots of new joiners. Annually only if you supplement with monthly 1:1s.

Q: What if I disagree with another lead's review of a shared report?

Admin can write a second review on the same employee. Both appear in My Performance with the reviewer's name attached.

Q: Can the employee write a self-review?

Not as a separate document in v4.5. A useful workaround: ask them to send you their self-assessment in chat before you write the official one.

Q: Can I edit a review after publishing?

Yes — open the review, edit, save. The change is recorded in the Audit Log. The employee will see the latest version.

Q: My team is brand new — what should I do?

Wait at least 3 months before the first formal review. Use weekly 1:1s in the meantime so when the review lands, no score is a surprise.
